# Brainstorming and reading notes related to Mills' Integrated Systems Ecology Lab project on changes in the structure and function of the Gulf of Maine ecosystem, with particular focus on apex predators and key forage species.

## Brainstorming

### What are we good at?
- Assessing the relationships between changing ecosystem conditions and fish species population dynamics, including growth/survival/productivity
- Understanding spatio-temporal patterns as a function of ecosystem conditions, with particular expertise in correlating occurrence of fish species with sea surface temperature
- Quantifying changes in mean/variability of ecosystem conditions, especially sea surface temperature

### What do we know about the GoM ecosystem?
- Rapid warming
- Changing ocenographic phenology (e.g., spring transition period, duration of summer and fall)
- Connections between rapid warming/changing oceanographic conditions and the base of the food web, especially relationships among oceanographic ecosystem conditions and zooplankton species like Calanus finmarchicus
- Correlations between oceanographic ecosystem conditions and distribution/abundance of adult marine fish and invertebrate species
- Mechanistic understanding of how changing temperatures might impact population dynamics of a *few* commercially important fish and invertebrate species and protected species Atlantic salmon

### What seems to be missing -- the middle?
It seems to me, we know a lot about the bookends of the Gulf of Maine ecosystem. With advancements in remote sensing, we can readily analyze oceanographic conditions to understand how mean/variability and phenology have changed through time. Given the prevalence of observations for higher trophic level, economically important fish and invertebrate species, we have also developed a fairly extensive understanding of how these changing conditions might influence species distribution and abundance. For a few of the species with more detailed life history data, we have been able to push this forward to build models of how changing temperatures have impacted species population dynamics (e.g., American lobster, Atlantic cod). With these two bookends, we understand that the structure of the Gulf of Maine ecosystem is fundamentally changing. However, we don't really have a great understanding (or I don't think we do) of how this structural change may translate into changing how the Gulf of Maine functions as we have limited knowledge about the middle of this ecosystem. In turn, we don't really have the ability to assess how structural changes may impact energy flows throughout the Gulf of Maine food web. 

This knowledge gap isn't entirely surprising as the middle of the foodweb consists of species with complex life histories and are some of the hardest to monitor using traditional fisheries sampling methods. How can we work to fill this gap?

## Notes from Fishes of the Gulf of Maine (Bigelow and Schroeder)

### Herring and herring-like fishes, order clupeiformes
**Atlantic herring**
*Habits*
- Marine, coastal pelagic species, often occurring in shallow waters, and predominantly in schools of hundreds to thousands of individuals
- "Obligate schoolers" (e.g., always doing it) found in "polarized schools" (e.g., coordinated movements in the same direction)
- Along with schooling behavior, other dominant behavior is diurnal vertical migration in response to light intensity
- Preferred temp ranges of 5-9 degC for adults, juveniles 8-12 degC, and below 4 degC or above 16 degC can result in physiologic stress.

*Food*
- Facultative, zooplanktivorous filter feeders
- Species change by availability, largely a function of season, and age. In the spring, barnacle larvae or cladocerans are dominant, then larger zooplankton and euphausiids take over later in the year. 
- Young herring (larvae to age 1): winter = small copepods (Psuedocalanus minutus) and copepodid stages, spring = larger copepods (small Psuedocalanus elongatus) and copepodid states). As fish get bigger, move towards calanus finmarchicus and temora longicornis.
- Adult herring (age 2 +): Transition to feeding nearly exclusively on euphausiids, chaetognaths, calanus finmarchicus, shrimp)

*Spawning*
- Wide range in spawning depths throughout its range, from shallow (0-5 m) to deep (200-300 m). Occurs in the spring and the fall, generally on shoals and ledges 8-40 km offshore in hard subrate/rocky/cobble areas with relatively strong currents. 
- Throughout it's range, somewhere some herring are spawning during each month of the year.
- Fall =  major spawning event in Gulf of Maine from August through November. 
- Spring = smaller spawning event

*Early life history*
- Pelagic larval stage relativel long (3-11, mean 6 months)
- Juveniles are generally 90-125 mm by the end of the first year of life.
- Age 1 herring 10-150 mm
- Age 2 herring 190-200 mm

*Seasonal movements*
- Within the GoM, and especially Georges Bank, appears to be three main movements: (1) late-summer early autumn migration to spawning areas, (2) quick move to over-winter areas to
- Movement is more common for adults than for juveniles southern/warmer water regions, (3) spring-early summer northward feeding migration


### Cods, family gadidae
**White hake**
*Habits*
- Demersal, overlapp with red hake
- Seem to generally occupy deeper waters (150-300 m), with juveniles showing a slight preference for shallower waters and waters 4-10 degC
- All ages tend to shoal and occupy shallower waters in the sumemr season 

*Food*
- Less than 1 YO deed mainly on shrimp, mysids and amphipods
- Post juvenile (20-400 cm) generally feed on decapod shrimps and euphausiids
- Adults feed on bony fishes (Atlantic herring, and groundfish), euphausiids and squids

*Early life stages*
- Pelagic juveniles occuring regularly in Gulf of Maine and Georges Bank from May-June likely from spawning along Georges Bank, southern New England and Mid-Atlantic Bight areas. 
- Juveniles become demersal around 50-60 mm, occuring in depths > 50m
