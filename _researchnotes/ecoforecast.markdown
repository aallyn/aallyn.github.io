# Jacox at al. 2020 - Seasonal to interannual prediction of U.S. coastal LMEs
**Introduction**
- Just like weather forecasts, there is an increasing need for ocean and marine ecosystem forecasts to support proactive management and conservation decisions (Payne et al. 2017, Tommasi et al. 2017a). Taking this proactive approach instead of a reactive one can help buffer against negative impacts of "poor" ocean conditions and maximize potential benefits during "good" conditions (Tommasi et al. 2017a, Hobday et al. 2016, 2018). Along with changes in trends and variability, such tools are particularly useful for extreme events, including marine heatwaves (Holbrook et al. 2019).
- The difference between marine ecosystem forecasts and climate projections is largely a matter of time scales. Climate projections focus on long-term trends and potential scenarios to inform policy decisions and guide long-term planning efforts. In contrast, marine ecosystem forecasts operate over shorter timescales (seasonal to interannual) more relevant to resource users, managers, and reliant communities. There are a number of different ecosystem forecasting frameworks, including modeling approaches, used to provide this information. Across all of these frameworks, striving to use statistical models informed through a mechanistic understanding should increase the forecast skill (Cuddington et al. 2013).

**Marine ecosystem forecasting methods**
Generall, we can break forecasting methods into two groups: dynamical forecasting models and statistical forecasting models.
- Dynamical forecasting: Resolve non-linearities, handle issues of non-stationarity, and excel at forecasting novel conditions
- Statistical forecasting: Require long data timeseries, struggle to resolve non-linearities and handle non-stationarity issues. But, less reource intensive and not subject to model error biases like dynamical forecasting models

*Physical forecasting methods*
- Coupled global climate forecasts
Dynamic prediction system that encompasses multiple coupled components (e.g., atmosphere, ocean, sea ice and land), with potential for climate feedbacks and controls. Examples include the North American Multi-Model Ensemble (NMME, Kirtman et al. 2014). For interannual to decadal timescales, World Climate Research Program is leading the charge. Subseasonal (2 weeks to 2 months) are also being developed through National Weather Service Global Ensemble Forcast System, among others. These are generally coarse-scale models, and therefore they tend to struggle with fine-scale processes (freshwater discharge, tidal forcing, coastal upwelling). Though, they have been shown to produce skillfull predictions for many other important physical variables, including large-scale models of climate variability like the El Nino-Southern Oscillation and monthly SST anomalies for many LMEs in US and across the globe (Stock et al. 2015, Hervieux et al. 2017).

- Dynamically downscalled regional forecasts
In marine ecosystems, these forecasts generally use some fine-scale regional ocean model nested within a global climate model, where the forcing and boundary conditions used in the regional ocean model are supplied by the coarser-scale global climate model. These show promise for overcoming some of the limations of global climate models for resolving fine-scale processes. Though, they are inherently linked to the skill and bias present in the global climate model. Some of these exist in the Pacific, but still getting off the ground in the Nortwest Atlantic. This is mostly because the northwest Atlantic lacks a dominant interannual climate signal to support high predictive skill like the ENSO system in the Pacific. In the Northwest.

- Statistical downscaling and forecasting
These forecasting methods strive to find statistical relationships between the large-scale global climate models and some response of interest (for example the Gulf Stream variability or the NAO). Although they some beneficial charactersitics, they rely heavily on monitoring data to capture the relationship signal. Synoptic climatology and linear inverse modeling are two examples of statistical downscaling techniques. 

- Hybrid statistical/dynamical approaches
Most common hybrid method includes statistically correcting global fields (forcing, boundary conditions) of global climate models and then supplying these corrected fields to regional ocean mdels. 

*Biogeochemical and ecological forecasting methods*
- Dynamically downscaled regional forecasts
A few of the dynamic downscaled forecasting for physical variables have been extended to include biogeochemical variables, especially nutrients, phytoplankton and zooplankton. These models have a number of challenges to overcome, mostly because the information included in the finer-scale regional ocean model is more extensive than that available from the global climate model. 

- Statistical forecasts
This is the most common approach. It consists of four components:
1. Ability to skillfully forecast physical environment,
2. Predictable ecological response of changes to physical environment,
3. Communication and engagement with stakeholders (Payne et al. 2017, Dietze et al. 2018),
4. Matching forecast time scales with time scales of decision making processes (Zador et al. 2016)
When using this approach, need to account for the uncertainty in BOTH forecasts of physical environmental variables and uncertainty in ecological response-physical environment relationships.

**Mechanisms of predictability**
There are certain mechanisms that we might be able to leverage ot enhance forecast skill.
*Oceanic processes*
- Persistence
Ocean anomalies. Once they are formed, they tend to stick around for a while (weeks to months). This is particularly true of Pacific ecosystems over Atlantic ecosystems (Hervieux et al. 2017). In the north Pacific, persistence forecasts excel out to 3 months, and sometimes out to as much as a year. In the Atlantic, skill degrades after only 1 or 2 months. Across both ocean basins, skill depends on depth and skill tends to increase with increasing depth or when using depth integrated physical variables.

- Re-emergence
The re-emergence phenomenon captures how seasonal variations in the depth of the surface mixed layer influence the evolution temperatures as the water mass travels throughout the water column. 

- Ocean waves
Kelvin waves, which propogate and move quickly, as well as Rossby waves, which propogate and move slowly, both effect ocean currents, hydrography and can have long lasting impacts on ocean physics and biology. Although kelvin waves (and other "coastal trapped waves") could increase "downstream" predictability, generally operate at fairly fine spatial scales, which would not be agreeable with global climate models. Additionally, at least in the Northwest Atlantic it seems that advection is the dominant process governing the equatorward movement of anomalies. 

- Tropical-Extratopical connections
Although the atmosphere is less predictable than the ocean on seasonal to inter-annual time scales, known atmospheric patterns assocaited with large scale climate variability like ENSO can increase ocean predictability. 

- Sea-ice related processes in subarctic regions
Many regions show "freeze to melt" memories (e.g., sea ice extent/area/thickness can return to where it was in the previous season following retreat) and there are connections between sea-ice extent and oceanographic characteristics (e.g., sea ice area/thickness and cold pool extent.)
