NOAA COCA Project Ecology paper synthesising quantitative distribution models and qualitative vulnerability assessments to project marine species distributions under climate change published in PLOS ONE

May 7, 2020

After many years of work, our paper Comparing and synthesizing quantitative distribution models and qualitative vulnerability assessments to project marine species distributions under climate change was recently published in PLOSOne. As climate change continues to warm ocean waters, we are seeing many marine species shift their distribution and abundance patterns. These distribution and abundance changes can create many problems. For example, distribution shifts may change the availability of resources to dependent coastal communities. Distribution shifts also challenge our traditional management decision frameworks that use historical observations as good indicators of future conditions. This is increasingly problematic as climate change alters the structure and function of marine ecosystems, creating novel environmental conditions and biological communities. To meet these challenges, we need models that can accurately project species distribution and abundance under potential future conditions. The research community has responded to this need by developing a variety of tools, including quantitative species distribution models as well as qualitative expert climate vulnerability assessments. Each of these tools have their strengths and weaknesses. In this paper, we tried to compare the different tools to learn more about their potential uses and then synthesize them into one cohesive modeling framework to build from the strengths of each tool in the hopes of producing more accurate predictions of species distribution and abundance. We found that quantitative species distribution models captured relative biomass patterns fairly well, though their is room for improving the predictive skill of these models to reduce prediction error and better capture fine-scale spatial variability. Distribution model projections suggest the region will undergo considerable biological changes, especially in the Gulf of Maine, where commercially-important groundfish and traditional forage species are expected to decline as coastal fish species and warmer-water forage species historically found in the southern New England/Mid-Atlantic Bight area increase. The quantitative species distribution model projections only occasionally aligned with vulnerability assessment expectations, with agreement more common for species with adult mobility and population growth rates that showed low sensitivity to climate change. Although our blended approach tried to build from the strengths of each method, it had no noticeable improvement in predictive ability over SDMs. Overall, this work rigorously evaluated the predictive ability of SDMs, quantified expected species distribution shifts under future climate conditions, and tested a new approach for integrating SDMs and vulnerability assessments to help address the complex challenges arising from climate-driven species distribution shifts.
