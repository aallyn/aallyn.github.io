Fisheries in the Northeast U.S. are incredibly economically, ecologically and culturally important. The sustainability of these critical marine resources is being threatened by continued global climate change, with sea surface temperatures in the region warming faster than the majority of the world's oceans. Making smart, sustainable, forward-looking decisions within this dynamic environment requires integrating data across atmospheric, oceanic, ecosystem, and human dimensions to produce forecasts to inform decision making processes. Overall, [this project](https://www.nsf.gov/od/oia/convergence-accelerator/Award%20Listings/Track%20A%20Abstracts/A-6950-Kerr-MaineResearchInstitute.pdf) strives to design and develop a fisheries open knowledge network to meet these needs. As part of that overall project, we are specifically focusing on using a forecasting challenge to assess the predictive skill of a suite of species distribution modeling approaches, from novel machine learning models to state-of-the-art spatio-temporal statistical models. Through this work, we hope to be able to better understand model strengths and weaknesses, examine the predictive skill of each modeling approach, and identify promising avenues for model development to advance model predictive skill. 

*Project collaborators*  
This project brings together scientists the Gulf of Maine Research Institute, Cornell University, Rutgers University, the NOAA Northeast Fisheries Science Center and Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory.

*Project funding*  
Funding for this project is provided by the NSF Convergence Accelerator Phase I program.
