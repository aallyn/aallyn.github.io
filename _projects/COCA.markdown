Marine species distributions are shifting in response to warming ocean temperatures, triggering complex ecological, conservation and management challenges. The GMRI COCA climate vulnerability and adaptation in fishing communities project aims to understand these coupled natural-human system dynamics within the Northeast U.S. Continental Shelf Large Marine Ecosystem (NES LME), a region experiencing extraordinary ocean warming. Within this larger project, I lead the ecological analysis that seeks to meet three objectives: 1. Project the distribution and abundance of important fish species given anticipated sea surface temperatures from global climate models 2. Compare projections from quantitative distribution models with qualitative expert assessments and then blend the two pieces of information together to create a more accurate and robust projection of future species distributions 3. Summarize projected distribution changes for specific fishing communities within the NES LME. The results from this analysis were published in [PLOS ONE](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/authors?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0231595). Since completing the ecological analyses, we are striving to integrate projected species distribution changes into an economics model that translates changes in relative biomass into changes in fisheries profits. Through this translation, the integrated ecological-economic analysis will help us provide resource users and managers the information needed to anticipate challenges and identify potential adaptation pathways as coastal communities face climate-driven changing marine ecosystem conditions.

*Project collaborators*  
This project brings together scientists the Gulf of Maine Research Institute, the NOAA Northeast Fisheries Science Center, and the NOAA Earth System Research Laboratory.
