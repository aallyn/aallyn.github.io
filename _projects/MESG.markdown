Distribution shifts are one of the most widely reported biological consequences of climate-driven warming across marine, freshwater and terrestrial ecosystems, as species move to track their preferred environmental conditions to ensure their growth, productivity, and survival. Species’ unique responses to these changing environmental conditions can create novel biological communities, in turn, altering the structure and function of marine ecosystems, including predator-prey interactions. The potential for such transformations is particularly acute in rapidly warming marine ecosystems, such as the Gulf of Maine, which has warmed faster than most of the world’s oceans during recent decades. In this study, we plan to assess whether recently developed joint species distribution models can identify and detect changes in predator-prey co-occurrence patterns within the Gulf of Maine ecosystem, with a particular focus on two iconic species within the region, Atlantic cod and American lobster, and their primary prey and predator species. Overall, we hope this work provides new insights into how predator-prey relationships have changed under considerably different warming trends. As ocean waters continue to warm, accounting for species-species relationships will be critical to providing more accurate species distribution model predictions and a more holistic understanding of how species-specific responses could create novel biological communities and alter traditional predator-prey relationships.
