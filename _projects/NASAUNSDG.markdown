Climate change is creating novel ecosystem conditions, challenging our traditional decision making processes, which have relied on historical observations as good indicators of future conditions. To support the ability of stakeholder’s to make sustainable, forward-looking decisions within these dynamic systems, we need models that can accurately predict species distribution and abundance across a range of time scales from near-term forecasts (seasonal, annual and multi-annual time scales) to long-term climate projections (decadal, mid-century to end-of-century time scales). Although considerable efforts have focused on model development and application, our understanding of the predictive skill of these models, and more specifically, how accurate we can expect model predictions to be, remains incredibly limited. In this project, we will work to fill this knowledge gap and assess the predictive skill of distribution models in ecosystems experiencing unique responses to climate change.   

*Project collaborators*  
This project is a collaboration with scientists from the Gulf of Maine Research Institute, San Diego State University, the University of Washington, the NOAA Southwest Fisheries Science Center and the University of California Santa Cruz.

*Project funding*  
Funding for this project is provided by NASA's Research Opportunities in Space and Earth Sciences (ROSES) A.8 Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 14 Life Below Water program.
