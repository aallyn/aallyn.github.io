**The Gulf of Maine Coastal Ecosystem Survey**
Despite its ecological and economic importance, there are many questions concerning the structure and function of the coastal Gulf of Maine Ecosystem. The Gulf of Maine Coastal Ecosystem Survey Project sought to increase our understanding of this critical region. In 2011 and 2012, I worked for the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service as a contract employee tasked with developing a Gulf of Maine Integrated Ecosystem Research Program, modeled after the highly successful Bering Sea Integrated Ecosystem Research Program and the Gulf of Alaska Integrated Ecosystem Research Program. As part of this work, I helped convene two workshops on the Gulf of Maine ecosystem structure and function. During these workshops, it became very clear that we needed to gain a better understanding of ecosystem dynamics within the coastal waters of the Gulf of Maine. After pulling together a team of collaborators and coming up with a survey and analysis plan to meet these research needs, I lead a proposal submitted to the State Wildlife Grant Competitive Program. After receiving funding, we completed an integrated, ecosystem survey in July 2014, 2015 and 2016. I then [lead the analysis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/324274508_THE_GULF_OF_MAINE_COASTAL_ECOSYSTEM_SURVEY_PROJECT_END_REPORT) to model the distribution and abundance of key communities within the coastal Gulf of Maine Ecosystem.

**Movement and habitat use of Great shearwaters in the Gulf of Maine**
Great shearwaters are one of the most abundant seabirds within the Gulf of Maine during the summer months, however, we know relatively little about their movements and habitat use. Working with researchers from [NOAA Stellwagen Bank National Marine Sanctuary](https://stellwagen.noaa.gov/science/shearwater13.html) and the [U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service](https://www.fws.gov/refuge/maine_coastal_islands/), we strived to fill these knowledge gaps. We captured Great shearwaters at sea using bait and dipnets, collected blood and feather samples and deployed satellite transmitters on captured individual. Using locations from tagged individuals, we assessed Great shearwater habitat use relative to oceanographic characteristics within the Gulf of Maine ecosystem throughout the summer. [Our analysis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/316624635_Movements_and_foraging_habitats_of_great_shearwaters_Puffinus_gravis_in_the_Gulf_of_Maine) showed a considerable amount of individual variability in habitat selection, however, generally, individuals spent more time foraging around the rim of the Gulf of Maine in shallower waters where bathymetry was more steeply sloped. Additionally, foraging locations appeared to be related to chlorophyll concentrations and water temperatures, though the specifics of these relationships varied depending on where an individual was captured (e.g., in the northeaster Gulf of Maine vs. the southwest Gulf of Maine). 

**Kittlitz’s murrelet population dynamics, habitat use and diet - University of Massachusetts Amherst Master's work** 
The Kittlitz’s murrelet is small pursuit-diving seabird that breeds within the waters of Alaska and eastern Siberia. Low population numbers, patchy distributions within dynamic habitats, and puzzling nesting habits have limited our ability to answer basic ecological questions concerning this rare seabird species. To help answer some of these questions, I worked with the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service in Prince William Sound, Alaska during the summers of 2008 and 2009 to complete at-sea surveys, and collect blood and feather samples and deploy radio transmitter tagged on captured adults. Using the data collected during these summers, we assessed the current Kittlitz’s murrelet Prince William Sound population size, identified landscape variables characterizing Kittlitz’s murrelet habitat use, and exampled Kittlitz’s murrelet diet and foraging behavior. Along with [updating estimates](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/260458850_Distribution_abundance_and_population_trends_of_the_Kittlitz's_Murrelet_Brachyramphus_Brevirostris_in_Prince_William_Sound_Alaska) for this important core population of Kittlitz’s murrelets, this work expanded our understanding of Kittlitz’s murrelet habitat use [relative to landscape features](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/273168782_Assessing_a_paired_logistic_regression_model_of_presence-only_data_to_map_important_habitat_of_the_rare_Kittlitz's_Murrelet_Brachyramphus_brevirostris) and [oceanographic conditions](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/260460034_Relationships_among_Kittlitz's_murrelet_habitat_use_temperature-depth_profiles_and_landscape_features_in_Prince_William_Sound_Alaska_USA).
